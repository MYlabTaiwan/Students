# Research Lab Expectations and Performance Framework

## Introduction

Welcome to the research lab. This document explains how we work together and what is expected from students in the group. The goal is not to control your behavior, but to help you grow into an independent, confident researcher. Research training is different from taking classes: it requires self-motivation, responsibility, and clear communication. These expectations are here to make the lab a fair, safe, and productive place for everyone. If something is unclear, you are always encouraged to ask.

---

## 1. Ownership and Intellectual Growth

You are expected to take responsibility for your own research project and learning. This means thinking carefully about your work, planning next steps, and reflecting on results. Over time, you should rely less on direct instructions and make more decisions on your own. A major goal of research training is to become deeply knowledgeable in your topic and to develop your own scientific ideas. Discussions with the advisor are meant to help refine your thinking, not replace it. Learning when to work independently and when to ask for help is an important part of your development.

---

## 2. Professional Commitment and Work Habits

Research in this lab is treated as a professional responsibility, similar to a full-time job. The expected effort is roughly full-time, though the exact schedule can change depending on experiments and deadlines. Research progress is more important than course grades for long-term success. Being present in the lab or office helps teamwork and learning from others. Sometimes, experiments or deadlines require work outside normal hours. Flexibility is part of research life.

---

## 3. Initiative and Problem-Solving

Research often involves uncertainty and obstacles. You are expected to actively look for solutions rather than waiting for instructions. This includes reading the literature, testing ideas, and asking others for input. If you are stuck for more than a short time, it is important to reach out rather than stopping work. When discussing problems with your advisor, try to bring possible ideas or questions. Regular reading of research papers helps you understand your field and improves your ability to solve problems.

---

## 4. Ethical Conduct and Research Integrity

Honesty and integrity are essential in research. All data must be collected, analyzed, and reported truthfully. Making up data, changing results, or copying others’ work is not acceptable. Safety is also very important. You must follow all safety rules, wear required protective equipment, and take safety training seriously. Good research takes care and patience. Shortcuts that risk accuracy or safety are not acceptable.

---

## 5. Collaboration and Lab Culture

The lab is a shared working environment. Everyone is expected to treat others with respect and professionalism. Lab members are teammates, not competitors, and helping each other is encouraged. It is okay not to know something and to ask questions. No one is expected to be an expert in everything. Everyone also shares responsibility for keeping the lab organized and running smoothly, including routine tasks and basic maintenance.

---

## 6. Communication and Reliability

Clear and regular communication is necessary for good research progress. You are expected to give regular updates on your work, usually weekly or semi-monthly. Meetings should be attended on time and with preparation. Responding to work-related messages in a reasonable time is important. Feedback from the advisor or others is meant to help you improve. Even if you do not agree with every suggestion, you are expected to consider it carefully and respond respectfully.

---

## 7. Evaluation and Feedback

Your progress is evaluated continuously, not only at the end of the year. Regular meetings, reports, completed milestones, and your level of independence all help show how you are doing. Sometimes, more formal reviews are used to reflect on progress and set goals. Evaluation is meant to help you understand where you are and how to improve, not to create stress or competition.

---

## 8. Addressing Difficulties and Expectations Not Being Met

If problems arise, the first step is always discussion and clarification. Many issues can be solved by adjusting plans or expectations. In some cases, a clear improvement plan may be used to help you get back on track. If problems continue for a long time, this may affect funding or the advising relationship. Serious issues related to ethics or safety may lead to immediate removal from the lab and possible formal action by the institution. Your professionalism and attitude also affect how strongly the advisor can support you in the future.

---

## Closing Statement

Research is a team effort that depends on trust, effort, and respect. Everyone’s work affects the whole group. By following these guidelines and communicating openly, you help create an environment where everyone can learn, contribute, and succeed.

If you are unsure about any expectation, please ask early rather than later.
